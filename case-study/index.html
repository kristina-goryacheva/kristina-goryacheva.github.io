Case Study

Stress Guide app:
new Measurement Result

The goal of this case study is to show the redesign process of the key screen of the Stress Guide app - Measurement Result.

Beginning with a challenge

When I joined KENKOU in October 2018, the company was in the transition state. The Stress Guide app designed in 2017-2018 struggled to meet user's needs and expectations, its fundamental usability was challenged and the overall design language looked outdated.

The old version of the Stress Guide Measurement result screen (2018)

The Goal

Our goal was to rethink and improve the existing Measurement Result Screen to resolve customer's confusion and create an engaging and easy-to-understand user experience, by transforming hard-to-understand scientific measurement results into valuable and meaningful data.

My role in the project

Inside of the Kenkou design team I collaborated with UX Designer Simone Sollmann. I worked on every stage of the design process of Stress Guide app from initial concept forming and user research to delivering the final design solutions. My responsibilities included:

User research

As the product was new and the user database was quite small, we didn’t have clear data on who our users are, what their expectations are and how they use the app. To collect the necessary user data with the constraints we had it was decided to use following user research methods.

/User Personas

One of the key things we had to do is to understand our user’s goals. We defined the most important users to target, grouping them into three main User Personas:

Tech enthusiast
Anxious
Stressed

Creating User Personas helped us to:

  • Understand the goals and motivations of our users;
  • Determine what the Stress Guide app should behave, feel and look like;
  • Help to keep the design centered on users and engage the empathy into the design process.
/User Testing

We hoped that the user testing would reveal the crucial problems of the app. We tested the existing Stress Guide app with 5 participants.

Our main goals were:

  • Collect as much relevant data as possible to get the overall idea of how the users use the app;
  • Define the user’s pain points;
  • Gather the honest user feedback about the app (visual and experience).

The outcome of the user testing revealed lots of valuable data. Aside from other minor problems, major 4 pain points were the following:

1. Overcomplicated data

The measurement results were hard to understand. Users mentioned the complexity of the data. They didn’t understand if the results were good or bad.

“It all looks cool and scientific but I don’t understand the majority of it and I’m too lazy to read through the explanations here”

-User Testing participant

2. Hard-to-read content

Majority of the users didn’t read long explanation texts. They saw the words they didn’t understand and very quickly lost the interest in “decoding” of the measurement result.

3. Confusion

User’s were confused with the names of the indexes and numbers they showed. They couldnt coorrelate the data they saw with the way they felt.

4. Missing value

Participants mentioned that they felt lost if the result were bad. They missed some guidence or advice on how they could improve it.

“I see that my result in the end in not so great.. so what should I do aboit it now?”

-User Testing participant
The first user testing session revealed the main crucial problems of the app and helped us to shape the vision of the improved version of the measurement result screen.
/Customer Journey Map

Early on, it was important to understand the different factors that may influence user experience. Using the outcomes of previous user research, we conducted a Customer Journey Map workshop to draw our company’s goals and user’s emotions into the picture.

Getting to the final solution

Gathering together all the data we got from our user research, we started to shape the new look of the Stress Guide app. In the context of the Measurement Result screen a key design challenge emerged:

How do we make the measurement result engaging and understandable for all our users?

A major reason our users struggled with measurement results was because of overcomplicated data visualization with abstruse explanations enriched with terminology that the average user hardly uses in their everyday life. We had to think of the solution which will solve this problem.

Primary goals that formed my design strategy:

  • Simplify data representation (both visual and copywriting);
  • Try a new approach in the delivering of the measurement result;
  • Add a recommendation engine based on the results to provide quick stress relieving exercises.

Prototyping

We decided to approach the problem from a different perspective and introduce a more playful, easy to understand solution that will help the users to instantly understand their measurement results without bombarding with overcomplicated data.

Early sketches of the new measurement result screen.
/Shapes and forms

We had to consider the complexity of the index explanations itself. It was important to convey to the user the concepts of “Stress Index” and “Recovery ability”. Diagrams and graphs were perceived as something overcomplicated and users struggled to read them, so we decided to go for something new. Playing around shapes and forms that are more familiar to the users.

Experimenting with Stress Index visualization
/Imagery and color

We wanted to reduce the user’s pain of grasping the measurement results and decided to go with imagery and color coding, but still wanted to keep it clean and simple. The familiar color pattern tells the result by itself. Providing this sort of visual hints users will be able to instantly understand their physical state.

Experimenting with color of indexes visualization

Final design concept

Throughout many and many of iterations and user testings we came up with the final look of the measurement result screen that solves all the main problems our users had.

/Imagery and color coding

Illustrations of the “Stress Index” and “Recovery ability” made it easier to understand what the values actually mean and in combination with color coding simplified the information perception.

/Short summary

By providing a short and simple summary of the user’s measurement result we reduced the confusion.

/Exercises for instant help

The crucial part of our design decisions were not only to give them an overall understanding of their stress status, but also recommend instant help exercises. This increases the value of the product itself.

Testing the final design desicions

I conducted the final user testing to make sure our concepts and design decisions work after all the changes that had been made.

As a result not a single participant mentioned difficulty understanding the measurement results. The screen resonated well with participants confirming our intuition around designing for simplicity, using imagery and colors for delivering complex ideas.

“Color is very important. I see blue and instantly understand that my results are good.”

-User Testing participant

The Outcome

Users found the new design engaging and understandable. The recommended exercises were received very well. Overall, the redesign of the Stress Guide app on iOS and Android has had a positive impact on the user experience.

/What I’ve learned

Working on the improvement of the Stress Guide app I learned a lot about the team work and do’s and don’t in the product development process. Going through mistakes, ups and down, I understood the importance of the “user-centered” approach and empathy at every stage of the development.

Being involved in every step of the product development, working closely with engineers, data science, and content teams boosted my knowledge of the Product Design process.
